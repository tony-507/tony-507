Altitude

Here we explore the construction of altitude of a line. More specifically, given a line OA, we find B such that \(OA\perp AB\). There are three ways to do so.

The first method is to extend OA to OAC with OA = AC, and then find the perpendicular bisector BD of OC. This has been discussed in the section of perpendicular bisector, so it won't be discussed here.

The second method employs converse of pythagoras theorem, i.e. we construct a right angle by Pythagorean triple.

  1. Fix a small length \(r\). Find C on OA such that AC = \(3r\).
  2. Draw arcs with radius \(4r\) centered at A and \(5r\) centered at C respectively. Denote the intersection of the two arcs by D.
  3. AD is the desired altitude of OA at A.

The proof of this construction is trivial. We can also use other Pythagorean triple for construction.

\( (3r,4r,5r)\) is a Pythagorean triple, so by converse of Pythagoras theorem, \(\angle BAO = \frac{\pi}{2}\).


The last method uses angle in semi-circle.

  1. Fix a point B that does not lie on OA. Draw a circle centered at B passing through A. Denote the intersection of the circle with A other than A by C.
  2. Connect CB and extend it to meet the circle again. Denote the point to meet again by D.
  3. The line AD is the desired altitude of OA at A.
