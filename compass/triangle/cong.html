Congruence

In proving congruence of triangles, we have five reasons:

  1. SSS: three equal sides
  2. SAS: two equal sides and equal angle in between
  3. ASA: two equal angles and equal side in between
  4. AAS: two equal angles and one of the remaining side equal
  5. RHS: right-angled triangles with equal hypotenuse and a pair of equal side

Below we illustrate them by construction. That is, we show that the construction is unique up to isometry.

In SSS, we have three lines with length \(a,b,c\) respectively.

  1. Connect the three lines into one straight line ABCD, where AB = \(a\), BC = \(b\) and CD = \(c\).
  2. Draw circles centered at B and C with radius AB and CD respectively. Denote the intersection of the two circles by E.
  3. \(\Delta ABE\) is the only possible triangle with side length \(a,b,c\).

The construction is intuitive and originates from Euclid's Elements. This also shows the triangle inequality.



In SAS, we have two lines OA and OB with length \(a,b\) respectively and \(\angle AOB = x \) is fixed. This case is rather trivial as the only possibility is to join AB.



In ASA, we have two angles and a straight line in between. This is also trivial as we can simply extend other two lines until they meet at a point.



In AAS, we are given two angles and a straight line, one of whose angle is one of the given angles. The construction can be seen by shifting the parallel lines to form a triangle.



In RHS, we are given a right-angled triangle with a fixed base and a fixed hypotenuse. We can Pythagoras theorem to find the remaining side and then apply SSS. Another way is to construct altitude at O.
