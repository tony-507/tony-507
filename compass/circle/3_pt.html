Circle from 3 points

Given 3 noncollinear points \(A,B,C\), we can find a unique circle from those 3 points. This is actually the same steps as before.

  1. Connect AB and BC.
  2. Construct perpendicular bisectors of AB and BC. Label their intersection as O.
  3. O is the desired center of the circle.

Algebraically, the cartesian equation of a circle is $$ x^2 + y^2 + Ax + By + C = 0.$$ If we have three points \(A(x_1,y_1), B(x_2,y_2), C(x_3,y_3) \), we have the system $$ \begin{cases} x_1A + y_1B + C & = -(x_1^2+y_1^2)\\ x_2A + y_2B + C & = -(x_2^2+y_2^2)\\ x_3A + y_3B + C & = -(x_3^2+y_3^2)\\ \end{cases}$$

The determinant of the system is $$\begin{align*} \begin{vmatrix} x_1 & y_1 & 1\\ x_2 & y_2 & 1\\ x_3 & y_3 & 1\\ \end{vmatrix} & = \begin{vmatrix} x_1 & y_1 & 1\\ x_2-x_1 & y_2-y_1 & 0\\ x_3-x_1 & y_3-y_1 & 0\\ \end{vmatrix}\\ & = (x_2-x_1)(y_3-y_1) - (x_3-x_1)(y_2-y_1). \end{align*}$$

If the determinant is zero, then $$ \frac{y_3-y_1}{x_3-x_1} = \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1},$$ so \(A,B,C\) are collinear. This implies, by contrapositive, 3 noncollinear points determine a circle uniquely.
