Inscribing equilateral triangles

We know how to find the circumscribed circle of a triangle. Here we do the reverse process: Given a circle and a point \(A\) on the circle, can we find an equilateral triangle inscribed in the circle? There are several ways to deal with the problem.

The first approach uses angles in alternate segment. If we have an equilateral triangle \(\Delta ABC\), then the angles that \(AB\) and \(AC\) make with the tangent at \(A\) should both be \(60^o\). We then connect the lines to form the desired triangle.

  1. Construct tangent to the circle at \(A\).
  2. Construct \(60^o\) from both LHS and RHS of the tangent from \(A\).
  3. Extend the two angles to meet the circle at \(B,C\) respectively. The triangle \(\Delta ABC\) is equilateral.

Another approach to solve the problem is to draw a circle with radius equal to the side length of the triangle. If the circle given has radius \(r\), by cosine rule $$x = \sqrt{2r^2(1-\cos(120^o))} = \sqrt{3}r.$$ This gives an easy way to find the triangle.

  1. Draw a circle with radius \(\sqrt{3}r\) centered at A.
  2. Label the intersections of the two circles by \(B,C\) respectively. Then \(\Delta ABC\) is the desired triangle.
